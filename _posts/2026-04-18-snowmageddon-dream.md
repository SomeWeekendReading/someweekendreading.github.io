Another New England winter has come &amp; gone.  Well&hellip; _almost._  


## The Spirit of Winters Past  

<a href="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-dream-icefall.jpg"><img src="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-dream-icefall-thumb.jpg" width="400" height="300" alt="A New England icefall: artesian water exiting a mountainside under pressure, then frozen in place" title="A New England icefall: artesian water exiting a mountainside under pressure, then frozen in place" style="float: right; margin: 3px 3px 3px 3px; border: 1px solid #000000;"></a>
Here in New England, we take winters seriously.  Not as seriously
[as we did 45+ years ago when I moved here](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Northeastern_United_States_blizzard_of_1978),  
but still pretty seriously.  

There are a number of beautiful things to be seen in winter that you just don't see in
warmer places, or in colder but flat places like the US midwest.  Here, for example, is
what is sometimes called an icefall: artesian water normally forced out of mountain rocks
by pressure, frozen in place in winter.  This leads to _ginormous_ icicles hanging from
what looks like an ordinary rock face; see the person in the lower right for scale.  

<a href="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-dream-frost-flowers.jpg"><img src="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-dream-frost-flowers-thumb.jpg" width="400" height="300" alt="Frost flowers growing from a grating over a storm drain in front of Ch&acirc;teau Weekend" title="Frost flowers growing from a grating over a storm drain in front of Ch&acirc;teau Weekend" style="float: right; margin: 3px 3px 3px 3px; border: 1px solid #000000;"></a>
At the other end of the size scale, there are delicate little
[frost flowers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frost_flower).  Here are some forming from
the unlikely source of a storm drain in front of Ch&acirc;teau Weekend.  As moist air from
the storm drain is vented up, it hits the grate and collapses like faery magic on cold
iron, leaving behind these very pretty little flower-like structures.  

They only last a few hours, usually destroyed by sunlight, more moist air, or &ndash; in
this case &ndash; being driven over by cars.  

<img src="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-dream-globe-0.jpg" width="400" height="167" alt="Mahan @ Boston Globe: Comparing 2026 to 'Snowmageddon' 2015" title="Mahan @ Boston Globe: Comparing 2026 to 'Snowmageddon' 2015" style="float: right; margin: 3px 3px 3px 3px; border: 1px solid #000000;">
<a href="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-dream-globe-0a.jpg"><img src="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-dream-globe-0a-thumb.jpg" width="400" height="282" alt="People walking in deep channels cut in snow in downtown Boston, 2015" title="People walking in deep channels cut in snow in downtown Boston, 2015" style="float: right; margin: 3px 3px 3px 3px; border: 1px solid #000000;"></a>
This last winter has been&hellip; _interesting._  The venerable _Globe_ compares 
winter 2026 to the "Snowmageddon" of 2015 <sup id="fn1a">[[1]](#fn1)</sup> (but does not
make the comparison to the blockbuster Blizzard of 1978; too antique, I guess).  

In a series of memorable consecutive blizzards in 2015, 8 feet of snow fell between 2015-Jan-24 and 
2015-Feb-22.  Here's a dramatic picture from the venerable _Globe_, showing people walking
around downtown in 2015.  They're not actually walking in chest-deep snow, but they _are_
walking in chest-deep, narrow channels cut in the snow with snowblowers and shovels.  If
you were a kid, you probably couldn't see out the sides.  This went on for&hellip; an
annoyingly long several weeks.  

<a href="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-dream-mt-snow-1.jpg"><img src="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-dream-mt-snow-1-thumb.jpg" width="400" height="300" alt="Earth moving equipment managing a multistory tall pile of snow" title="Earth moving equipment managing a multistory tall pile of snow" style="float: right; margin: 3px 3px 3px 3px; border: 1px solid #000000;"></a>
<a href="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-dream-mt-snow-2.jpg"><img src="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-dream-mt-snow-2-thumb.jpg" width="400" height="533" alt="Earth moving equipment in front of a mountain of snow, topped by foolishly daring MIT students" title="Earth moving equipment in front of a mountain of snow, topped by foolishly daring MIT students" style="float: right; margin: 3px 3px 3px 3px; border: 1px solid #000000;"></a>
<a href="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-dream-mt-snow-3.jpg"><img src="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-dream-mt-snow-3-thumb.jpg" width="400" height="300" alt="View from a 3rd story lab window, looking UP at the top of the snow pile, where earth moving equipment was working" title="View from a 3rd story lab window, looking UP at the top of the snow pile, where earth moving equipment was working" style="float: right; margin: 3px 3px 3px 3px; border: 1px solid #000000;"></a>
Then there's the problem of how to _dispose_ of snow.  You can only shove it to the side
of the road with a plow for so long, lest you leave the buildings and sidewalks entombed
until spring.  So some snow gets put on trucks and taken&hellip; _somewhere._  

It used to be dumped in the harbor, to melt.  This turns out to be a terrible idea: snow
scraped off streets is full of salt, asphalt, gravel, broken glass, and multiple other
things that make it no fun for humans to touch and certainly nothing suitable for putting
in harbor waters you're trying to clean up.  So, enter "snow farms": usually
municipally-owned vacant lots or even sacrificial parking lots where snow gets piled up.
And by "piled up", I mean _really_ up.  

There was one such snow farm next to my lab back in 2015.  Here are some views of it:  
- The first shows just a huge pile of snow and ice, with some yellow earth-moving
  equipment on top.  That thing is _big._  To give you some idea of scale, this was taken
  out a 3rd story window, and the big earth moving machine is at about eye level.  
- The second picture shows a much smaller earth mover on the ground, but the hill crowded
  with some very silly MIT students who wanted to climb the mountain that had been built
  in a parking lot.  It's silly because: it's slippery, making falls likely; there can be
  voids underneath that collapse &amp; swallow you; and it's full of broken glass and bits
  of metal that will slice you up.  I did see one extremely dangerous video of a person
  skiing on a similar snow farm, which is dangerous enough that cops intervened to prevent
  serious injury.  (For once, I was on the cop side.)  
- The third picture shows the snow mountain at what was probably its peak height.  Note
  that we are looking out a 3rd story window, and we are looking _up_ at the peak.  That
  snow farm has built a mountain about 4 stories tall, and as big as a parking lot that
  spans maybe half a city block.  And it was one of dozens such snow farms.  

We had an office betting pool about when it would melt.  As I recall, nobody one because
nobody would be so extreme as to guess it melted in July.  But, July it was.  It left
behind mounds of broken glass, asphalt, a couple shopping carts, and even a fire hydrant:
all stuff that got scraped off by giant snowplows, operated none too carefully.  


## Sometimes Life Imitates Art

Looking at that snow farm back in 2015, I had a fantasy that when it finally melted, there
would be a car under it.  In that car would be a body, preserved by the cold&hellip; and
that's the start of a murder mystery to find out who killed whom 6 months ago.  

Again the venerable _Globe_ points out that sometimes life imitates
art. <sup id="fn2a">[[2]](#fn2)</sup>  

As one of the snow farms in neighboring Somerville illustrates, sometimes you do find
actual cars when the tons of snow above them melts.  Needless to say, these cars are not
in exactly good condition after all that.  

Internet wags notwithstanding, it turns out that the city of Somerville actually knew all
about this.  The 6 cars involved were all junk vehicles, awaiting being towed for scrap in
a city parking area.  (And there's another story about why the city had that parking area,
involving eminent domain, resistance to building a Cop City, legal fights with the
original owner leading to millions more having to be paid to seize it in eminent domain,
and so on.)  But the snow happened quickly, a snow farm was urgently needed, and there was
no time to tow the cars, so&hellip; they were like those wooly mammoths frozen in Russian
tundra.  

No bodies in the cars so far, at least not as reported in the venerable _Globe_.  

So that's a relief.  Sometimes life shouldn't imitate art _too_ carefully.  


## The Weekend Conclusion  

<a href="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-early-april-snow.jpg"><img src="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-early-april-snow-thumb.jpg" width="400" height="300" alt="A brief snow in April in New England" title="A brief snow in April in New England" style="float: right; margin: 3px 3px 3px 3px; border: 1px solid #000000;"></a>
<a href="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-floral-frost-on-roof-one-april-morning.jpg"><img src="{{ site.baseurl }}/images/2026-04-18-snowmageddon-floral-frost-on-roof-one-april-morning-thumb.jpg" width="400" height="300" alt="A floral frost pattern on a black metal roof and skylight window, one April morning in New England" title="A floral frost pattern on a black metal roof and skylight window, one April morning in New England" style="float: right; margin: 3px 3px 3px 3px; border: 1px solid #000000;"></a>
Look, I love winter.  

The dark, the cold, the snow&hellip; it's all beautiful.  Just look at these 2 pictures of
the grounds here at Ch&acirc;teau Weekend.  The first is a freakish snowstorm in early
April this year, for which the snow accumulation an hour later was exactly zero.  The
second is a beautiful frost pattern seen on a metal roof and skylight, reminiscent of 
[an arts &amp; crafts floral rug design in the style of William Morris](https://www.google.com/search?q=william+morris+carpet+design).
This didn't last either; again like faery magic it dissolved at the dawn sun.  

With enough snow, you can make my winter daydream come true: underneath tons of snow,
melting months later, a car appears.  (Though, admittedly, with a pedestrian explanation.
It had better be pedestrian, because nobody's driving _those_ cars ever again.)  

The snow farms are kind of ugly after they've been there a few months, but amusing nonetheless.
